OCR Accuracy Tips

OCR is powerful, but getting good results requires understanding how it works and what affects accuracy. Here's what actually makes a difference:

  • Image Quality is Everything: Higher resolution scans produce dramatically better OCR results. If you scanned at 150 DPI (dots per inch), expect mediocre accuracy. Scan at 300 DPI or higher for best results. Blurry, low-resolution images may produce gibberish or miss text entirely. The clearer the original scan, the better the outputâ€”there's no way around that.
  • Language Selection is Critical: Always choose the correct language from the dropdown. If your document is in Spanish but you select English, accuracy will tank because the OCR engine is looking for the wrong character patterns and word structures. For mixed-language documents (like English with Spanish quotes), you may need to run OCR multiple times with different language settings and combine the results manually.
  • Clean, Printed Text Works Best: OCR engines are optimized for clear, printed textâ€”like typewriter output, laser-printed documents, or professionally published books. Handwriting, stylized fonts, decorative scripts, or poor contrast (light gray text on white backgrounds) reduce accuracy significantly. If you can barely read it yourself, OCR won't do much better.
  • Processing Time Varies Wildly: OCR is computationally intensive. A single page can take 10-30 seconds depending on your device's speed, image resolution, and text complexity. A 10-page document might take 3-5 minutes total. This is normal for browser-based OCRâ€”close other tabs and applications to speed things up, but don't expect instant results like other PDF tools.
  • Beta Feature Means Occasional Hiccups: This tool is in beta, which means it's powerful but not perfect. Expect occasional inaccuracies, especially with complex layouts (multi-column text, tables, footnotes), decorative fonts, or documents with poor scanning quality. Always proofread the extracted text before using it for anything importantâ€”don't assume 100% accuracy.
  • Background and Contrast Matter: Documents with clean white backgrounds and dark black text produce the best results. Yellowed paper, colored backgrounds, faded ink, or watermarks confuse the OCR engine. If your scanned document has a noisy background, consider cleaning it up in an image editor before running OCR.

ðŸ“„ OCR vs. Regular Text Extraction: When to Use Each

Confused about whether you need OCR or just regular text extraction? Here's the difference and when to use each:

Use OCR when: Your PDF is a scanned image of a physical document (like a scanned invoice, receipt, or contract). If you can't select or copy text in the PDF, it's image-based and needs OCR. PDFs created from photos, screenshots, or scanner output always require OCR to extract text.

Use Regular Text Extraction when: Your PDF already has selectable textâ€”meaning you can click and drag to highlight words. If copy/paste works, you don't need OCR. Just use your PDF reader's built-in copy function or a standard text extraction tool. OCR is overkill and slower for PDFs with existing text layers.

Quick Test: Open your PDF and try to select some text with your mouse. If you can highlight and copy it, skip OCR. If clicking just selects the whole page as an image, you need OCR.

Pro tip: Some PDFs look like they have text but are actually scanned images with invisible text layers added by previous OCR. If the text looks weird when you copy it (wrong characters, garbled words), try running our OCR tool again with the correct language settingâ€”it might produce cleaner results than the embedded OCR layer.

Frequently Asked Questions

What'\''s the difference between OCR and regular text extraction?

OCR (Optical Character Recognition) analyzes images to recognize and extract text from scanned documents or image-based PDFs. Regular text extraction works on PDFs that already have selectable text layers. If you can copy/paste text from your PDF, you don'\''t need OCR.

Why is OCR so slow compared to other PDF tools?

OCR is computationally intensive because it must analyze every pixel in an image to recognize characters and words. Processing a single page can take 10-30 seconds depending on image size, quality, and your device'\''s performance. This is normal for browser-based OCR.

How accurate is the OCR text extraction?

Accuracy depends on image quality, text clarity, and language selection. High-quality scans with clear, printed text typically achieve 95%+ accuracy. Low-resolution images, handwriting, unusual fonts, or poor contrast will reduce accuracy significantly.

Can this tool recognize handwriting?

OCR engines like Tesseract are optimized for printed text, not handwriting. You may get partial results with very clear, legible handwriting, but accuracy will be poor. For best results, use OCR on typed or printed documents only.

What languages are supported?

This tool supports 100+ languages including English, Spanish, French, German, Italian, Portuguese, Chinese (Simplified/Traditional), Japanese, Korean, Arabic, Russian, and many more. Select the correct language from the dropdown for best results.

Why does the tool say "No text found" after processing?

This can happen if: (1) Your PDF already has selectable text (no OCR needed), (2) The page contains only images/graphics with no readable text, (3) Text quality is too poor for recognition, or (4) You selected the wrong language. Try adjusting the language setting or checking image quality.
